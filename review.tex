\begin{abstract}
Simulations in virtual reality (VR) offer a robust platform for acquiring new skills across diverse applications. The virtual environment (VE) facilitates the study of complex skills and user performance evaluation, enhancing our understanding of motor learning. Recent research suggests that haptic feedback in VR offers a great way to improve several aspects of motor learning, including timing, accuracy, and skill transfer. However, the exact impact of different levels of feedback fidelity on motor learning remains unclear.
In this paper, we conducted a systematic review to search for studies related to this research question. We then evaluated the feedback fidelity of each system involved in the studies using the Haptic Feedback Fidelity Framework by Muender et al. \cite{Muender2022HapticReality}, which we extended with a confidence score and the observed impact on motor learning to create a standardized basis for comparing the studies.
Our findings indicate that abstract haptic feedback, characterized by low fidelity and limited alignment with user actions, generally resulted in negative or negligible impacts on motor learning. Representational feedback, with moderate fidelity, typically produced positive outcomes, including decreased response times and enhanced performance. High-fidelity haptic feedback showed an overall positive impact on motor learning, particularly for tasks requiring ballistic movements or high precision, or involving a third dimension.

\end{abstract}
